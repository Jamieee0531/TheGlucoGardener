Ramadan Fasting Guide for Diabetic Patients in Singapore

Ramadan is the Islamic holy month of fasting (sawm). For Muslim patients with diabetes, fasting from dawn (Subuh/Imsak) to sunset (Maghrib) requires careful management to maintain safety. This guide supplements clinical advice — always consult your doctor before Ramadan.

== SHOULD DIABETICS FAST DURING RAMADAN? ==

Many people with Type 2 diabetes can safely fast during Ramadan with proper medical guidance. Fasting is generally not recommended for:
- Type 1 diabetes (high risk of DKA)
- Recurrent hypoglycaemia (low blood sugar episodes)
- Poor glucose control (HbA1c > 10%)
- Recent hospitalisation for diabetic emergencies
- Pregnant women with diabetes
- Patients on multiple insulin injections

Speak to your doctor or diabetes nurse educator at least 4-6 weeks before Ramadan to plan medication adjustments.

== BLOOD GLUCOSE THRESHOLDS: WHEN TO BREAK THE FAST ==

Break your fast immediately if blood glucose is:
- Below 4.0 mmol/L (hypoglycaemia risk)
- Above 16.0 mmol/L (hyperglycaemia risk)
- Below 3.9 mmol/L during the first few hours of fasting even without symptoms

Do not delay breaking the fast for prayer — your health takes priority. Islam permits breaking the fast (Rukhsah) when medically necessary.

Monitor blood glucose more frequently during Ramadan:
- Before Suhoor (pre-dawn meal)
- Mid-morning (2-3 hours into fast)
- Mid-afternoon
- Before Iftar (sunset meal)
- 2 hours after Iftar

== SUHOOR (PRE-DAWN MEAL) ==

Suhoor is the most important meal during Ramadan for diabetics. Eat as late as possible before Imsak (the start of fasting).

Good Suhoor foods (slow-release energy, low GI):
- Oats / wholegrain cereal with low-fat milk
- Wholemeal bread with egg or peanut butter
- Brown rice with vegetables and protein (chicken, fish, tofu)
- Barley water (unsweetened)
- Dates — 1-2 only (traditional, but high in sugar; limit quantity)
- Fruits: banana, apple, pear (avoid fruit juice — too fast absorbed)

Avoid at Suhoor:
- White rice in large portions
- Sugary drinks, Milo, condensed milk
- High-sodium foods (nasi lemak, instant noodles) — cause thirst during fasting
- Very oily foods (roti prata with curry) — cause sluggishness

Hydration at Suhoor:
- Drink 2-3 glasses of water
- Avoid caffeinated drinks (coffee, teh tarik) — increase fluid loss

== IFTAR (BREAKING FAST AT SUNSET) ==

Break fast gently — do not overeat.

Traditional: break with 1-3 dates and plain water (Sunnah). Dates are high GI but in small amounts are acceptable.

Iftar meal structure:
1. Break fast: 1-3 dates + water
2. Wait 10-15 minutes (pray Maghrib)
3. Light meal: soup, salad, vegetables first
4. Main meal: normal-sized portion of protein + carbohydrate + vegetables

Common Iftar mistakes for diabetics:
- Eating very large portions after a long fast (causes blood sugar spike)
- Drinking sugary drinks (Bandung, syrup drinks, Sarsi) — causes rapid glucose spike
- Eating fried foods in large quantities (bazaar Ramadan items: goreng pisang, spring rolls, curry puffs)
- Skipping the light starter and going straight to heavy main meal

Bazaar Ramadan tips:
- Goreng pisang (fried banana): high in fat and carbs. One piece occasionally, not a plate.
- Murtabak: large portion, high GI. Share with family.
- Ayam percik (grilled chicken): good protein choice. Limit the sauce.
- Ketupat with rendang: ketupat is high GI glutinous-style rice. Limit portions.
- Air sirap / Bandung: avoid or ask for no sugar / very little syrup.

== MEDICATION ADJUSTMENTS DURING RAMADAN ==

Always consult your doctor. General principles:

Metformin:
- Usually safe during Ramadan. Dose may be adjusted to Iftar and Suhoor timing.

Sulphonylureas (Gliclazide, Glipizide):
- Higher risk of hypoglycaemia. Dose and timing typically adjusted by doctor.
- May need to reduce dose or switch to modified-release formulation.

SGLT2 inhibitors (Empagliflozin, Dapagliflozin):
- Risk of dehydration and DKA during fasting. Doctor review essential.

Insulin:
- Significant dose adjustments required. Do not adjust insulin on your own.
- Doctor will advise on new timing and doses for Ramadan schedule.

DPP-4 inhibitors (Sitagliptin):
- Generally low risk of hypoglycaemia. Timing adjusted to Iftar.

Note: Blood glucose testing and taking medication (including insulin) does NOT break the fast in Islamic rulings according to major scholars and Singapore's Islamic Religious Council (MUIS).

== EXERCISE DURING RAMADAN ==

Exercise is still beneficial but needs timing adjustment:
- Best time: 1-2 hours after Iftar (blood glucose refuelled)
- Avoid strenuous exercise just before Iftar (risk of hypoglycaemia)
- Light walking after Tarawih prayers is acceptable
- Reduce intensity if feeling weak or dizzy
- Stay hydrated between Iftar and Suhoor

== RECOGNISING AND MANAGING HYPOGLYCAEMIA ==

Symptoms of low blood sugar (hypoglycaemia):
- Shakiness, trembling
- Sweating
- Dizziness, lightheadedness
- Confusion, difficulty concentrating
- Palpitations (fast heartbeat)
- Hunger
- Pale skin

If these symptoms occur during fasting: check blood glucose immediately. If below 4.0 mmol/L, break the fast.

Treatment for hypoglycaemia (15-15 rule):
1. Take 15g fast-acting carbohydrates (3-4 glucose tablets, half a glass of fruit juice, 3 teaspoons of sugar in water)
2. Wait 15 minutes
3. Re-check blood glucose
4. If still below 4.0 mmol/L, repeat
5. Once normalised, eat a small snack (biscuits, bread) to maintain level

== HYPERGLYCAEMIA DURING RAMADAN ==

If blood glucose rises above 16.0 mmol/L, break the fast and seek medical advice.

Signs of diabetic ketoacidosis (DKA) — seek emergency care immediately:
- Nausea and vomiting
- Abdominal pain
- Fruity breath
- Rapid breathing
- Confusion

== SINGAPORE RESOURCES ==

Patients can seek pre-Ramadan diabetes review at:
- Polyclinics (CHAS subsidy available)
- SingHealth Diabetes Centre
- National University Hospital Diabetes Centre
- TTSH Diabetes Service
- KKH (for gestational diabetes)

Many polyclinics offer Ramadan-specific diabetes counselling. Ask your doctor or diabetes nurse educator for a pre-Ramadan medication review appointment.

MUIS (Islamic Religious Council of Singapore) has issued guidance that insulin injections and blood glucose testing do not invalidate the fast.
